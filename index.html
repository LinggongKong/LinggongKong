I am a Ph.D. candidate in Public Policy & Public Administration at Auburn University, with fields in international relations and comparative politics. My research examines how great-power rivalry, regional security dynamics, and domestic political culture interact to shape foreign policy choices and democratic governance.

Substantively, I focus on U.S.–China relations, China’s grand strategy, and security politics in Northeast Asia. Methodologically, my work combines qualitative case studies, comparative analysis, and theory-driven empirical research to speak to both scholarly debates and policy audiences.

My recent and forthcoming work appears in Politics & Policy, European Foreign Affairs Review, and SN Social Sciences, and I regularly contribute policy commentary to outlets such as The Conversation, Asia Times, and The Diplomat.

Research

Research Interests

My research centers on the politics of great-power competition, with a particular focus on U.S.–China relations and the evolving security architecture of Northeast Asia. I am interested in how grand strategy, alliance politics, and domestic political constraints interact to shape state behavior in a changing international order.

In comparative politics, I study political culture and democratic governance, especially how historical legacies and cultural traditions influence regime performance, social mobilization, and public policy. My dissertation bridges these interests by examining the cultural underpinnings of democratic outcomes in Protestant and Confucian contexts.

Research Themes

  • U.S.–China strategic competition and foreign policy making
  • China’s grand strategy and relations with major powers and regions
  • Security dynamics and escalation risks in Northeast Asia
  • Political culture, religion, and democratic performance
  • Elite leadership, civil–military relations, and institutional stability

Current Projects

When to Test Missiles: North Korea’s Ballistic Missile Launch Timing

This project analyzes how international political conditions, regional security dynamics, and domestic considerations shape North Korea’s timing of ballistic missile tests. It seeks to clarify when and why missile launches are used as signaling tools in crisis and non-crisis periods. (Under review)

Trump’s Second Term and the Liberal International Order

This work develops an integrative theoretical framework to assess how a second Trump administration would interact with, challenge, or reshape the liberal international order. It evaluates the order’s resilience and potential pathways of transformation. (Under review)

Tenure of Military Chiefs of Staff Across the Globe

Co-authored with Peter B. White and Salah B. Hammou, this project investigates cross-national variation in the tenure of military chiefs of staff. It explores how regime type, institutional design, and civil–military relations affect leadership stability and turnover. (In progress)

Nonprofits and Voter Turnout Among Asian Americans

Joint work with Xiaoyu Qin examines how nonprofit organizations shape voter mobilization across generations of Asian Americans, with attention to community engagement, issue framing, and identity-based appeals. (In progress)

Assessing U.S.–China Strategic Competitiveness

This project systematically compares the strengths and weaknesses of the United States and China in their ongoing rivalry, spanning economic capabilities, technological innovation, military power, alliance networks, and ideational influence. (In progress)
